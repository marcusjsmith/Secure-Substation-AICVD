Secure Substation CVD overview

A Cisco Validated Design reference for building secure, segmented, and observable utility substations across large transmission, medium distribution, and compact field-site profiles.

Why read this CVD

Substations are no longer isolated serial environments. Modern utility sites carry SCADA, management, telemetry, PMU, video, and remote-support traffic across shared IP infrastructure while still needing deterministic operations, cyber separation, and auditable security controls.

This CVD gives engineering, OT operations, and security teams a common reference for turning those requirements into a repeatable architecture. It shows how to structure the substation edge, how to separate critical services, where to apply inspection, and how to size the WAN for large, medium, and small sites.

Reduce design ambiguity Align network, OT, and security teams around one validated blueprint instead of one-off substation builds.
Protect critical operations Preserve SCADA reachability while adding segmentation, firewall policy, application recognition, and inspection where it matters.
Plan before deployment Relate traffic types, bandwidth assumptions, and product choices before configuration begins.
Improve audit readiness Map architecture decisions to zoning, access control, monitoring, and operational evidence expected in regulated utility environments.

Why Cisco

Cisco brings the substation WAN, industrial LAN, security policy, and OT visibility layers into one architecture. That matters because secure substations need more than a router, a switch, or an IPS rule in isolation; they need those controls to work together across the full lifecycle.

Rugged OT infrastructure Industrial routers and switches such as IR8340, IR1101, IE9300, IE3100, and IE3500 support hardened deployment models for utility environments.
SD-WAN and segmentation Cisco SD-WAN provides centralized overlay policy, service VPN separation, encrypted transport, and scalable operations for many substations.
Security policy at the edge Zone firewall, application-aware policy, NBAR, and Snort IDS/IPS capabilities can be aligned to the same traffic paths shown in the architecture.
OT visibility Cisco Cyber Vision adds asset, protocol, and anomaly context so policy decisions are informed by what is actually running in the substation.

What the CVD establishes

The Secure Substation CVD brings together ruggedized routing and switching, Cisco SD-WAN segmentation, Cyber Vision visibility, and policy-driven security controls into a repeatable substation blueprint. It is organized around the network behaviors utilities need to preserve: deterministic SCADA reachability, resilient WAN transport, bounded management access, monitored OT assets, and controlled paths for higher-bandwidth services such as PMU and physical-security video.

The design uses three deployment tiers so the same principles can be applied without overbuilding smaller sites: dual-router large substations, single-router medium substations, and compact IR1101-based small substations. Each tier is tied back to service VPN separation, WAN sizing, Cyber Vision telemetry, and security policy placement.

Service separation SCADA, MultiService, and Management traffic are treated as distinct service VPNs with different trust, QoS, and inspection requirements.
Substation profiles Large, medium, and small designs map platform choices to expected device scale, traffic volume, and resiliency needs.
Operational visibility Cyber Vision sensors and center connectivity provide asset discovery, protocol awareness, and anomaly context across OT zones.
Security alignment NERC CIP impact categories, NGFW policy, NBAR application recognition, and Snort IPS are positioned against the architecture.

Design outcomes

Standardize the substation edge Use a common routing, switching, and SD-WAN pattern while still sizing the design for large, medium, and small facilities.
Protect critical OT paths Constrain SCADA, management, and multiservice flows with segmentation, zone-based firewalling, application matching, and IPS inspection where required.
Plan WAN capacity Relate traffic types such as DNP3, PMU, CCTV, enterprise access, and management telemetry to practical bandwidth and QoS assumptions.
Connect design to deployment Carry the same architecture into platform references and configuration examples for routers, switches, and security functions.

SD-WAN design

Use this page to position Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN in the Secure Substation CVD: controller roles, overlay construction, service VPN segmentation, and the security policy capabilities applied at the rugged WAN edge.

Cisco SD-WAN architecture in the Secure Substation CVD

The SD-WAN design creates a secure overlay between substation routers and control-center headends while keeping SCADA, Management, and MultiService traffic separated. IR8340 and IR1101 routers act as substation WAN edges; the controller stack supplies orchestration, identity, route exchange, and central policy.

Architecture model

Transport independence: The SD-WAN overlay runs across MPLS, private fiber, broadband, LTE, or mixed utility WAN transports. Each router authenticates into the fabric and builds encrypted tunnels to approved peers.

Service segmentation: The CVD maps OT and operations traffic into separate service VPNs or VRFs. SCADA remains tightly constrained, Management is limited to approved operations systems, and MultiService carries higher-bandwidth workloads such as PMU and physical-security video.

Substation fit: Large substations use resilient IR8340 edge pairs, medium substations use a single IR8340 edge, and small substations use IR1101 with IE3100/IE3500 access switching.

SCADA service VPN DNP3, IEC 61850 MMS, RTU/IED supervision, and control-center master traffic with strict zone firewall and QoS treatment.
Management service VPN Device administration, telemetry, syslog, SNMP, NetFlow/IPFIX, certificate operations, and remote support paths.
MultiService VPN PMU, CCTV, AMI, and approved utility applications that need bandwidth without collapsing into the SCADA zone.
Management plane

SD-WAN Manager

Also known as: vManage in earlier Cisco SD-WAN naming.

  • Central GUI and API for device onboarding, templates, policy design, monitoring, troubleshooting, and software lifecycle.
  • Creates and distributes feature templates for IR8340 and IR1101 routers, including VPNs, interface roles, routing, QoS, and security policy.
  • Provides operational views for tunnel health, alarms, application visibility, policy counters, and events that feed operations workflows.
Identity plane

SD-WAN Validator

Also known as: vBond in earlier Cisco SD-WAN naming.

  • Authenticates devices and controllers during onboarding so only authorized routers join the overlay.
  • Helps edge routers discover the controller fabric and establish secure control-plane connectivity.
  • Supports certificate-based identity and serial/chassis validation, reducing the risk of unapproved edge devices joining substation services.
Control plane

SD-WAN Controller

Also known as: vSmart in earlier Cisco SD-WAN naming.

  • Maintains secure control sessions to WAN edge routers and exchanges overlay routing information using OMP.
  • Distributes centralized control and data policies that determine segmentation, path selection, route reachability, and tunnel behavior.
  • Allows the CVD to enforce common topology intent across large, medium, and small substations without manually stitching every tunnel.
Segmentation

Service VPN and zone design

Role: Keep substation workloads separate before inspection policy is applied.

Security policy starts with the overlay model. SCADA, Management, and MultiService traffic should be isolated in separate service VPNs or VRFs, then mapped to trusted zones at the edge router.

  • Restrict SCADA flows to known masters, RTUs, and approved protocols.
  • Keep management access on dedicated paths with MFA/jump-host controls upstream.
  • Apply QoS and path policy per service so PMU or video traffic cannot starve SCADA supervision.
Substation pattern SCADA VPN to SCADA zone, Management VPN to management zone, MultiService VPN to video/PMU/application zone, with explicit inter-zone rules.
Firewall

Zone-based firewall and NGFW policy

Role: Enforce permit, deny, log, and inspect decisions between WAN, SCADA, management, and multiservice zones.

Cisco SD-WAN security policy can centrally define firewall behavior and push it to supported IOS XE SD-WAN edge routers. In this CVD, the firewall is the control point between overlay VPNs and local OT zones.

  • Permit only approved source and destination pairs for DNP3, HTTPS, SSH, syslog, SNMP, NetFlow/IPFIX, PMU, and video flows.
  • Deny and log unknown or cross-zone traffic that does not match the substation communication baseline.
  • Use application-aware matches where supported so rules are not limited to static port numbers.
Example control WAN SCADA zone to substation SCADA zone: permit DNP3 only from approved masters to approved RTUs, deny and log all other traffic.
IDS / IPS

Snort intrusion detection and prevention

Role: Inspect selected flows beyond 5-tuple firewall rules and detect or block protocol misuse.

Supported SD-WAN edge routers can use Snort-based intrusion detection and prevention through Cisco security policy. For substations, this is most useful on high-risk WAN or inter-zone paths where the firewall permits the protocol but the payload still needs inspection.

  • Detect suspicious activity in approved flows such as DNP3, Modbus/TCP, IEC 61850 MMS, HTTPS, or management traffic.
  • Run in alert/monitor posture during tuning, then move selected controls to prevention where false positives are understood.
  • Use custom OT-focused rules when utility-specific SCADA behavior needs tighter validation than generic signatures.
Example inspection Permit DNP3 between known endpoints, then inspect with Snort to alert or drop malformed frames, unexpected function codes, or traffic outside the approved master-outstation pattern.
Application ID

NBAR and application-aware policy

Role: Classify applications so firewall, QoS, and inspection decisions can use application identity.

NBAR provides application recognition for SD-WAN policy and visibility. In a substation, it helps distinguish expected OT protocols from generic or unknown traffic that happens to use an allowed port.

  • Match application names or families in policy sequences where supported by the platform and release.
  • Align application recognition with QoS so SCADA and PMU receive predictable treatment during WAN congestion.
  • Use visibility data to validate the traffic profiles documented in the Architecture section.
Example match Application = DNP3, source = control-center master subnet, destination = substation RTU subnet, action = allow plus inspect.

Design guardrails

  • Validate release support: Security features vary by IOS XE SD-WAN release, platform, and license. Confirm IR8340 and IR1101 support before final policy baselines.
  • Separate control and data planes: Controller connectivity is required for overlay operation, but OT payload policy should be designed per service VPN and zone boundary.
  • Use staged enforcement: Build policy from visibility first, then move from monitor/log to enforce/drop on carefully scoped OT paths.
  • Coordinate with Cyber Vision: Use Cyber Vision asset and protocol visibility to refine SD-WAN application, firewall, and IDS/IPS rules.

NBAR & Snort policy design

Apply application-aware classification and deep packet inspection after the zoning model is understood. This section shows how NBAR, ZBFW, NGFW policy, and Snort IPS work together on the SD-WAN edge.

Application-aware inspection on SD-WAN

The Secure Substation CVD uses application-aware security on Cisco SD-WAN edge routers (IR8340, IR1101): NBAR identifies OT and IT protocols so policies can match traffic precisely, and custom Snort IPS rules perform deep protocol inspection—critical for SCADA protocols such as DNP3 that generic port-based ACLs cannot fully protect.

Context: Zone-based firewall (ZBFW) and service VPN segregation (SCADA, MultiService, Management) define trust boundaries. Within and between those zones, Next Generation Firewall (NGFW) features on IOS XE SD-WAN combine NBAR classification with Snort inspection and vManage-centralized security policies—aligning to NERC CIP electronic access and monitoring expectations without blocking legitimate OT operations.

Packet flow — SD-WAN edge router (IR8340 / IR1101)

Representative northbound SCADA path (e.g. DNP3 master → substation RTU over the SCADA service VPN). Use the overlay toggles to highlight ZBFW pass/drop or the full Inspect path through Snort IPS.

All paths: Base diagram showing permit forwarding, ZBFW deny, and Snort IPS drop. Select an overlay to highlight a specific policy action.

ZBFW pass: Policy action Permit or Pass — packet is allowed through the zone-pair rule and bypasses Snort (no deep inspection). Forwarded to SD-WAN policy and egress.
ZBFW drop: Policy action Deny — packet is dropped at the firewall match; never reaches Snort or egress.

Inspect path: Policy action Inspect — ZBFW hands the flow to Snort IPS (UTD) for protocol inspection (e.g. custom DNP3 rules). On pass, traffic continues to SD-WAN and egress. On IPS match, packet is dropped and logged.

Deep inspection

Custom Snort rules (IPS)

Role: Protocol-level intrusion prevention and anomaly detection on traffic selected by security policy—not only “allow/deny by port.”

Snort runs as an IPS service on the SD-WAN router (UTD / Snort integration on supported IOS XE releases). Custom rule sets extend the stock signature base so utilities can enforce OT-specific expectations: valid DNP3 function codes, permitted master–outstation relationships, and detection of malformed or unauthorized application payloads.

  • DNP3: Inspect application data on TCP/UDP (e.g. port 20000) for protocol violations, unexpected function codes, or traffic that does not conform to your utility’s SCADA profile—beyond what a simple 5-tuple ACL allows.
  • Other OT protocols: Modbus/TCP, IEC 61850 (MMS/GOOSE where supported), and IEC C37.118 (PMU) can be targeted with tailored Snort preprocessors or community/utility rules where deep inspection is required on WAN or zone boundaries.
  • Policy actions: Alert, drop, or rate-limit per vManage security policy; logs feed SOC/SIEM for correlation with Cyber Vision and syslog.
  • Operations: Test custom rules in monitor-only mode first; tune false positives against known master scan schedules and unsolicited responses.
Example use case On the SCADA service VPN, permit DNP3 between approved control-center and substation RTU subnets, but use Snort to alert or block DNP3 frames with disallowed function codes or anomalous lengths—reducing risk from compromised hosts that still use “correct” ports.
Classification

NBAR (application recognition)

Role: Identify applications and protocols in flow traffic so SD-WAN security policies can match on application name, not only IP, port, or protocol number.

Network-Based Application Recognition (NBAR) performs stateful deep packet inspection to classify traffic into application categories. On SD-WAN edges, NBAR attributes are used by the NGFW / unified security policy engine in vManage to build rules such as “permit DNP3,” “deny unknown,” or “inspect Modbus.”

  • OT protocol matching: NBAR includes industrial and SCADA-related classifiers (e.g. DNP3, Modbus, IEC 61850 MMS, BACnet, and others depending on IOS XE/SD-WAN release). Policies reference these names instead of brittle port-only matches.
  • NGFW integration: Security policy sequences match application or application-family, then apply firewall, IPS (Snort), or advanced malware actions—consistent across hub and spoke routers from a single vManage template.
  • QoS alignment: The same NBAR classification can drive QoS marking (e.g. prioritize DNP3 and C37.118 PMU streams) on WAN interfaces—matching the traffic profiles in this CVD.
  • Visibility: Application visibility in vManage and NetFlow/IPFIX exports supports capacity planning and proves which OT apps traverse each service VPN.
Example policy match Match: Application = DNP3 · Source = SCADA_master_zone · Destination = substation_RTU_zone · Action = Inspect (Snort) + Allow.
Match: Application = unknown · VPN = SCADA · Action = Deny + Log.
SD-WAN NGFW

How Snort and NBAR work together

On IR8340 / IR1101: NBAR classifies the flow; the security policy decides whether to permit, deny, or hand off to Snort for protocol inspection—implementing defense-in-depth on OT WAN paths.

In the Secure Substation architecture, NBAR is the classifier and Snort is the inspector. Port-based rules remain a fallback, but OT deployments should prefer application-based rules for protocols like DNP3 that may share infrastructure with other services.

  • Design pattern: Segregate traffic into service VPNs (SCADA, MultiService, Management) → use ZBFW zone-pairs per SRD → apply vManage NGFW policies with NBAR application matches → enable Snort IPS (including custom OT rules) on high-risk zone-pairs or Internet/WAN boundaries.
  • DNP3 end-to-end: Classify with NBAR as DNP3 → permit only between approved zones → Snort validates protocol behavior → QoS preserves supervisory traffic under congestion.
  • Change control: Centralize policy in vManage feature templates; lab-test Snort rule updates against PCAPs from your masters and RTUs before production push.
  • Complement Cyber Vision: Cyber Vision discovers assets and anomalies on the LAN; SD-WAN NBAR/Snort enforces and inspects what is permitted across the WAN and between zones.
Deployment note Confirm IOS XE SD-WAN release, UTD/Snort licensing, and NBAR classifier support for your exact OT protocol mix on IR8340 and IR1101 before finalizing utility security baselines. Custom Snort rules require a defined update and regression process—treat them like any other BES cyber system change under your CIP change-management program where applicable.

Related CVD controls

  • Zone-based firewall: On-router ZBFW between SCADA, process, and WAN zones (see configuration dialogs on IR8340/IR1101 in the traffic-flow diagram).
  • Service VPNs: SCADA, MultiService, and Management VRFs/VPNs limit where NGFW policies must be applied—scope Snort and NBAR rules to the correct overlay.
  • Traffic flows view: Use SCADA Traffic and management overlays to visualize paths that security policies protect.

Hover components for role & capabilities

WAN Manager (vManage)

Orchestration & operations

Function: Central management plane for the SD-WAN fabric—single pane for design, provisioning, monitoring, and lifecycle of all edge routers and policies.

Capabilities: Device templates and feature templates, ZTP onboarding, RBAC, alarms and reporting, software upgrades, multitenancy, REST/API integration, and troubleshooting (OMP routes, tunnels, SLA).

Validator

Identity & certificate authority

Function: Fabric PKI that issues, renews, and revokes identity certificates so only authorized controllers and WAN edges can join the overlay.

Capabilities: Certificate enrollment and whitelisting, OTP/bootstrap validation, serial/chassis binding, and coordination with vManage for controlled onboarding—prevents rogue devices from establishing control or data plane adjacency.

Controller

Control plane hub

Function: Maintains persistent DTLS/OMP control sessions to every SD-WAN router; distributes overlay reachability, policies, and tunnel endpoint (TLOC) information across the fabric.

Capabilities: OMP route/policy exchange, hub for mesh and hub-and-spoke topologies, HA clustering for resilience, and real-time signaling that drives IPsec/DTLS data tunnels between spokes and headends.

SCADA traffic

OT supervisory traffic between control-center masters and substation RTUs/IEDs, carried on the segregated SCADA service VPN.

  • DNP3 TCP/UDP (e.g. port 20000) polling and unsolicited responses from masters to field devices; primary protocol in this CVD for transmission and distribution SCADA.

Management traffic

Operations, monitoring, and administration between NMS, jump hosts, and field infrastructure—typically modest bandwidth but required for visibility and secure remote support.

  • SSH Interactive CLI and scripted automation to routers, switches, and appliances.
  • HTTPS WebUI, REST APIs, and certificate-based management sessions (e.g. Catalyst Center, device GUIs).
  • Syslog Centralized event and alarm forwarding for correlation and SIEM ingestion.
  • NetFlow / IPFIX Sampled flow records exported to collectors for capacity planning and troubleshooting.
  • SNMP Poll-based metrics and traps for fault, performance, and inventory monitoring.

MultiService traffic

Corporate / multi-service VRF paths for higher-bandwidth or non-SCADA OT workloads—often the dominant WAN consumers where PMUs and cameras are deployed.

  • CCTV IP camera video (RTSP/RTP, H.264/H.265) for physical security—continuous or event-driven streams from substation cameras to VMS/recorders.
  • PMU (IEEE C37.118) Synchrophasor UDP/TCP streams at 30–60 samples per second; per-PMU throughput commonly 1–5 Mbps and can exceed traditional SCADA polling.
  • AMI data Advanced metering infrastructure backhaul (utility-specific protocols) from collectors or gateways to head-end systems.

Controller overlay (TLS)

Outbound control-plane sessions from every SD-WAN edge router to Cisco SD-WAN orchestration and validation services.

  • OMP Overlay Management Protocol over DTLS between vEdge/cEdge and controllers for route, policy, and tunnel state.
  • HTTPS / NETCONF vManage and Validator reachability; template and certificate lifecycle.
  • DTLS/TLS Encrypted control channels—not user or OT application payload.

IPsec Hub & Spoke

Site spoke routers build encrypted tunnels into headend hub routers across the SD-WAN fabric (hub-and-spoke overlay topology).

  • IKEv2 Key exchange and peer authentication between IR8340/IR1101 spokes and IR8340 headends.
  • ESP (AES-GCM) Encrypted encapsulation of overlay and/or underlay traffic per utility crypto policy.
  • SD-WAN IPsec Tunnel interfaces bound to vManage transport templates; hub terminates spoke overlays for routing and service VPN handoff.

Cyber Vision telemetry architecture

Embedded sensors, northbound paths, and sensor-to-center traffic over the SD-WAN fabric.

Architecture ports

Sensor → Center

Embedded Cyber Vision sensors on IE9300 / IE3x00 switches send DPI-derived metadata and security events to the Cyber Vision Center over the SD-WAN fabric. Direction: substation → control center.

ServiceProto / portRole
Sensor telemetry & policyTCP 443 (TLS/HTTPS)Primary channel — asset inventory, flow metadata, events, certificate-pinned to Center
DNSUDP/TCP 53Optional — resolve Cyber Vision Center FQDN
NTPUDP 123Optional — time sync per utility policy

Path (large): IE9300 sensor → IR8340 → SD-WAN fabric → control-center firewall/NMS → Cyber Vision Center.
Path (small): IE3x00 sensor → IR1101 → hub → Center.
DPI observes OT locally (SPAN/embedded tap); it does not replace SCADA protocols.
